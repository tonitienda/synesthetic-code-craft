# Rocket science really is rocket science — Specs

## Working title

Rocket science really is rocket science

## Topic

A beginner-friendly explanation of why reaching orbit is hard, why rockets look and fly the way they do during launches, and why small changes in payload mass can cascade into much larger vehicle requirements.

## Audience

Curious viewers who watch SpaceX, NASA, ESA, Rocket Lab, Blue Origin, or other launch streams and want to understand the basic physics without prior orbital mechanics knowledge.

## Language

English.

## Target duration range

8–12 minutes.

## Purpose

By the end, the viewer should understand that a launch is not mainly about “going up”; it is about building enough sideways speed to keep falling around Earth. The viewer should also understand why the rocket equation makes mass so punishing, why engine performance matters, and why staging is not an arbitrary design choice.

## Required concepts

- Escape velocity and how it differs from orbital velocity.
- Delta-v as a velocity budget rather than simply top speed.
- The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation.
- Payload mass, propellant mass, dry mass, mass ratio, and payload fraction.
- A simulation-like explanation of payload mass causing more propellant, which causes more vehicle mass, which causes still more propellant.
- Specific impulse / effective exhaust velocity as the lever that makes propellant more useful.
- Gravity losses and atmospheric drag as reasons real launch requirements exceed a simple circular-orbit speed.
- Gravity turn / pitch-over: why rockets start vertical but bend toward horizontal flight.
- Staging: why two or more phases/stages beat one giant single-stage vehicle for most orbital launches.
- Launch stream vocabulary that helps viewers decode what they see: Max Q, MECO, stage separation, second engine start, fairing separation, orbital insertion.

## Non-goals

- Do not compare fuel chemistries in depth.
- Do not catalogue engine cycle types.
- Do not turn this into a history of rocketry.
- Do not derive every equation rigorously; use enough math to explain cause and effect.
- Do not cover interplanetary trajectory design except as a brief note about escape velocity and launch windows if useful.

## Tone

Calm, clear, and slightly playful: the phrase “rocket science really is rocket science” should feel earned, not gatekeeping. The video should make the complexity intuitive rather than mystifying.

## Visual direction

- Dark background, minimal geometry, high-contrast labels.
- Use an Earth curve, velocity arrows, and a rocket mass stack.
- Prefer animated ratios, bars, tokens, and curves over dense equations.
- Show the rocket equation as a machine: input payload and performance, output rapidly growing launch mass.
- Show launch trajectory as a transition from “up to clear air” into “sideways to orbit.”

## Constraints

- This folder is currently in specs/research only. Later phases should not be started until these docs are reviewed.
- Research claims should cite reliable sources, preferably NASA educational material or primary agency documents.

## Open questions

- Should the final video center on a fictional simplified rocket, a Falcon 9-like launch profile, or a fully abstract launch?
- Should escape velocity be framed as a common misconception first, or introduced after orbital velocity?
- How much numerical detail is appropriate for the target audience: rough values only, or one worked rocket-equation example?
