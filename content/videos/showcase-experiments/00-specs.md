# Showcase Experiments

## Purpose

Create a silent Motion Canvas material laboratory for choosing the visual language of
future technical videos. Individual studies repeat the same UI specimen and interaction
sequence so the material treatment—not different content—is what changes. Combination
studies then test whether different materials can communicate different system roles.

## Audience

Internal visual-design review for Synesthetic Code Craft.

## Format

- Twenty-four independent scenes: twelve material studies, three combinations, one
  complete material-system composition, five non-rectangular shape/motion studies, and
  two fixed-step Matter.js comparisons, followed by one 2.5D/Three.js rocket comparison.
- 1920×1080, dark background, restrained copy, generous spacing.
- No narration or factual teaching sequence.

## Material set

1. Glass — translucent, precise, cool, lightly refractive.
2. Bubble — brilliant plastic, rounded, buoyant, concentrated highlights.
3. Obsidian marble — dark stone, veined, heavy, luxurious.
4. Walnut — warm grain, crafted, grounded.
5. Brushed metal — directional grain, crisp, engineered.
6. Soft paper — layered fibres, matte, quiet, editorial.
7. Frosted glass — diffuse, private, architectural.
8. Smoked acrylic — dark, transparent, technical.
9. Luminous gel — alive, translucent, energetic.
10. E-ink — static, legible, declarative.
11. Copper circuitry — conductive, warm, connected.
12. Concrete — massive, stable, foundational.

## Combination set

1. Frosted glass boundary + luminous gel process.
2. Marble foundation + glass interface.
3. Smoked acrylic tool + copper signal.
4. Final system: concrete foundation, frosted boundary, smoked-acrylic terminal,
   luminous-gel process, copper flow, E-ink configuration, and paper annotation.

## Shape and physical-motion studies

1. Neural graph — spherical gel perceptrons connected by curved copper paths.
2. Token cache — paper tokens transform into gel state and enter a metal cache drum.
3. Rocket propulsion — metal cylinder, copper nose and fins, fire, and seeded smoke.
4. Water network — thick curved pipes, visible liquid flow, and a traveling droplet.
5. Physics playground — impact, collision, and pendulum/joint motion using deterministic
   Motion Canvas choreography as a baseline for evaluating a future physics dependency.
6. Matter.js physics — the same impact, collision, and pendulum categories simulated at a
   fixed 60 Hz and sampled by Motion Canvas.
7. Matter.js material stack — Bubble, Metal, and Marble panels mapped to distinct mass,
   restitution, and friction values before colliding with a Concrete ground surface.
8. Rocket dimension study — the same launch choreography applied to a continuous 2.5D
   silhouette and a procedurally modeled Three.js rocket with controlled studio lighting.

## Shared comparison choreography

Every scene must show the specimen:

1. appearing;
2. moving and changing size;
3. becoming active;
4. splitting into two related components;
5. reacting to an input;
6. showing active and inactive states together;
7. joining back into one component.

Object continuity is preferred over replacement. Motion should express the material:
bubble is springy, marble is heavy, wood is firm, metal snaps into alignment, paper
settles lightly, and glass glides cleanly.

Matter.js may drive interactions where simulation reinforces that identity: Marble and
Concrete use gravity-led landings, and dedicated comparison scenes expose rigid-body
behavior directly. Bubble split/join motion uses authored stretch, adhesion, recoil, and
exact settling so it reads as sticky without unwanted tumbling. Precise layout, membrane
spreading, and semantic morphs remain authored Motion Canvas choreography.

## Non-goals

- No narration, sound design, or educational claims.
- No bitmap textures or binary assets.
- No uncontrolled particle fields, realistic physics simulation, or permanent idle wobble.
- 3D is reserved for hero objects whose volume materially improves the explanation.
- This is a visual experiment, not a new reusable material framework yet.
